Theory

Front Foot Defense

  • The front-foot defense needs to be adopted when the ball is pitched on good length, in a straight line with the stumps. The purpose of this shot is to block out a potentially dangerous delivery, rather than to try and score off it.
  • The head and front shoulder should shift in the line of the ball, with the front leg coming right up to the pitch of the ball. Failing to do this could easily result into missing the shot altogether, with the ball going in between the bat and pad. The back leg should remain straight.
  • The bat should be just in front of the pads, with minimal distance between the in a straight line. The heel of the back-foot should be raised, and the position should be stilled, rather than following through.

Front Foot Drive

  • There isn’t much difference in how to play the front-foot drives from the defense, except for the point of impact of the bat on the ball, where the bat play is extended, and the result is a scoring shot, rather than a blocking one.
  • It is vitally important to get to the pitch of the ball, and keep the head still and in front of the vertical line of the pads. The weight should be shifted forward with the front knee bent a little, and the top hand firm, unlike the relaxed grip for a defensive shot. A drive is timed perfectly only if the top hand does most of the work, with the bottom hand used only to support. The bat should follow through in the line of the shot, and accelerate through a straight line.

Types of Drives

  • Off Drive: Played to a ball pitched just around the off stump. The bat should come down in a straight line in the direction of mid-off, to make sure that the ball is propelled toward that direction.
  • Cover Drive: Played to a ball pitched slightly wider outside the off stump. The bat should come down in the direction of extra cover, so as to hit the ball in that area.
  • Square Drive: Played to a ball pitched wider outside the off stump, in the direction square of the wickets.
  • On Drive: One of the most difficult drives to play, which is being eradicated from the modern game gradually, as not many exponents of it are left. Played between mid-on and mid-wicket.
  • Straight Drive: The most eye-catching of all drives, played straight down the ground.

The batsman may choose which drive to play on a particular good length ball, but emphasis definitely lies on getting to the pitch of the ball.

Good Length Ball

  • It makes the batsman unsure whether to play forward or backwards.
  • Often batsmen defend good length deliveries which are hitting the stumps or just outside the line.
  • With good length deliveries it can be hard to attack and keep the ball on the ground.
  • It is also risky to attack with a cross bat.
  • G/L Varies from “ height & reach “ of the batsman & also for Medium Pacers & Spinners

Backlift/Footwork

  • The back-lift should be anywhere between the WK & 2nd slip & at stumps high , this will help straight bat swing.
  • Lead with head & shoulder.
  • Step forward alongside the line of the ball - with your front toe pointing approximately in the direction of the stroke.
  • Front knee bent & back leg braced to transfer body weight.
  • Balance- Stability is required to execute the shot perfectly with control.

Downswing Impact

  • Produce a slow swing and strike the ball with soft hands.
  • Strike the ball late, underneath the eyes.
  • Finish the shot with a high elbow.
  • Follow through - for defensive shot there is no follow through.
